Quick definitions

Amaray-style case
The most common DVD case format, featuring a hinged cover and central hub; standard dimensions are approximately 7.5 × 5.35 × 0.43 inches (190 × 136 × 11 mm).
Slim case
A thinner variant, typically 0.28 inches (7 mm) thick, used to save shelf space and reduce shipping weight.
Multi-disc case
Designed to hold two or more discs, with additional hubs on the interior panels; thickness varies from 0.55 to 0.87 inches (14–22 mm).

DVD case dimensions and fit

Understanding dvd case dimensions is essential for shelving design, mailer selection, and artwork preparation. The standard Amaray-style DVD case measures 7.5 inches (190.5 mm) in height, 5.35 inches (135.9 mm) in width, and 0.43 inches (10.9 mm) in thickness. These dimensions accommodate a single disc and allow the case to fit neatly on most retail and home shelving systems. Slim cases reduce thickness to approximately 0.28 inches (7 mm), making them ideal for multi-title collections where space is at a premium.

Spine width is a critical consideration for dvd case printing. Standard cases offer a spine width of roughly 0.43 inches, providing sufficient area for legible text and small graphics. Slim cases, with their narrower spine, require careful typography to ensure readability. Multi-disc cases increase thickness to between 0.55 and 0.87 inches (14–22 mm), depending on the number of discs and hub configuration. When planning inserts, always verify the exact internal dimensions of your chosen case style, as tolerances can vary by manufacturer.

For shipping and storage, consider outer packaging dimensions. A standard case fits comfortably in a padded mailer measuring at least 8 × 6 inches, with additional clearance for protective padding. Bulk orders often arrive in cartons holding 50 or 100 units; confirm carton dimensions with your supplier to optimise warehouse racking and freight consolidation.

DVD case materials and durability

The choice of dvd case materials directly impacts durability, clarity, and environmental footprint. Most DVD cases are manufactured from polypropylene (PP) or polyethylene terephthalate (PET). Polypropylene offers excellent hinge strength and resilience to repeated opening and closing, making it the preferred material for cases that will see frequent handling. It is also less prone to stress-whitening at the hinge compared to some other plastics. PET provides superior optical clarity, which is advantageous when the case design relies on transparent panels to showcase interior artwork or disc labels.

When evaluating dvd case materials, consider scuff resistance and surface finish. High-gloss finishes enhance colour vibrancy but show fingerprints and scratches more readily. Matte or satin finishes offer a more subdued appearance and better hide minor abrasions. Recyclability is increasingly important; both PP and PET are widely accepted in recycling streams, though local facilities vary. Look for resin identification codes (PP is code 5, PET is code 1) moulded into the case to facilitate sorting.

Disc retention hubs are a critical component of disc case protection. Hubs should grip the disc centre hole firmly enough to prevent movement during transit, yet release the disc smoothly without excessive force that could crack the disc. Hub design varies: some use a simple button-style clip, while others employ a more complex multi-finger grip. Test samples under realistic conditions—simulate drops, vibration, and temperature fluctuations—to ensure the hub maintains its grip without deforming the disc.

DVD case selection checklist: Verify optical clarity for cover visibility, confirm hinge flexibility through 50+ open/close cycles, assess hub retention with sample discs, check for odour (some plastics emit volatile compounds), evaluate scuff resistance with abrasion testing, and confirm resin identification codes for recycling compliance.

DVD case printing and artwork workflow

Successful dvd case printing begins with meticulous file preparation. Artwork must account for bleed (typically 0.125 inches or 3 mm beyond the trim line), safe area (at least 0.25 inches or 6 mm inside the trim), and spine alignment. Spine text legibility is a common challenge: use a minimum font size of 8 points for sans-serif typefaces and ensure adequate contrast between text and background. Colour profiles should match the printing process—CMYK for offset lithography, RGB for digital proofing—and always request a printed proof before committing to a full run.

Templates are indispensable for dvd packaging design. Most dvd case manufacturers and printing services provide downloadable templates in Adobe Illustrator, Photoshop, or PDF format. These templates include precise guides for cover, spine, and back insert dimensions, as well as fold lines and hub clearance zones. Common errors include spine misalignment (text that wraps onto the front or back cover), insufficient resolution (images below 300 DPI appear pixelated), and incorrect colour mode (RGB files sent to a CMYK printer yield unexpected colour shifts).

Proofing is a multi-stage process. Begin with a digital soft proof to verify layout and typography. Follow with a printed proof on the actual substrate (coated paper stock) to assess colour accuracy and finish. Finally, assemble a physical dummy case with the printed insert to confirm fit, spine alignment, and overall appearance. This iterative approach minimises costly reprints and ensures that your custom dvd cases meet professional standards.

DVD case storage solutions and organisation

Effective dvd case storage solutions balance accessibility, space efficiency, and disc case protection. Shelving units designed for media typically feature adjustable shelves spaced 8 to 10 inches apart, accommodating standard DVD case heights with room for easy removal. For larger collections, consider modular shelving systems that expand vertically or horizontally as your library grows. Dvd storage box options include lidded bins for archival storage, open crates for frequently accessed titles, and drawer-style units that slide out for browsing.

Organisation methods vary by personal preference and collection size. Alphabetical arrangement by title is intuitive and simplifies retrieval. Genre-based organisation groups similar content together, making it easier to select a film for a particular mood or occasion. Director or actor-based sorting appeals to cinephiles who follow specific creators. Whichever method you choose, use dividers or labelled sections to maintain order and prevent cases from leaning or toppling.

Environmental factors affect long-term disc case protection. Store cases in a climate-controlled environment with stable temperature (ideally 15–21°C or 59–70°F) and relative humidity (30–50%). Avoid direct sunlight, which can fade printed inserts and degrade plastic over time. Dust accumulation is inevitable; periodically wipe cases with a soft, dry cloth and vacuum shelving to minimise particulate buildup. For archival collections, consider UV-filtering sleeves or storage in closed cabinets.

Dvd case replacement becomes necessary when hinges crack, hubs break, or cases become heavily scratched. Evaluate whether to replace the entire case or transfer the insert and disc to a new shell. Replacement cases are available in bulk from packaging suppliers, often at a fraction of the cost of original retail cases. Re-sleeving—placing the disc in a paper or plastic sleeve within a new case—is a cost-effective alternative for less valuable titles.

DVD case organisation checklist

  • Choose a primary sorting method: alphabetical, genre, director, or chronological.
  • Use dividers or labels to mark sections and facilitate browsing.
  • Ensure shelving is sturdy and appropriately spaced for case height.
  • Maintain stable temperature and humidity to prevent warping and fading.
  • Inspect cases periodically for damage and replace as needed.
  • Keep a digital inventory to track titles and identify gaps in your collection.

Wholesale, manufacturers, and bulk ordering

Lead times depend on production capacity, material availability, and shipping method. Standard lead times range from two to six weeks for stock items, with custom tooling or colour matching extending timelines to eight weeks or longer.

Sample approval is a critical step in the procurement process. Request samples of the exact case style, material grade, and colour you intend to order. Evaluate hinge durability by opening and closing the case at least 50 times. Check hub retention by inserting and removing a disc repeatedly. Assess optical clarity by holding the case up to a light source and inspecting for haze or discolouration. Verify that the case dimensions match your artwork templates and that any custom features (such as embossed logos or textured finishes) meet your design intent.

Packaging specifications for bulk shipments affect both product protection and logistics costs. Cases are typically packed in shrink-wrapped bundles of 10 or 25, then placed in corrugated cartons. Confirm the carton dimensions, weight, and stacking strength to ensure compatibility with your warehouse racking and freight consolidation plans. Discuss transit packaging with your supplier: additional corner protectors or pallet wrapping may be necessary for international shipments or long-haul trucking.

Supplier evaluation should encompass material certification, production consistency, and returns policy. Request documentation of resin grades and any relevant safety or environmental certifications (such as RoHS compliance for European markets). Consistency across batches is essential: colour, hinge tension, and hub grip should remain uniform from one production run to the next. Clarify the returns policy for defective units, including who bears the cost of return shipping and whether replacements are provided on a one-for-one basis or as a percentage credit.
